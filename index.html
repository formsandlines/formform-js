formform 0.2.0b

Concept and Development by Peter Hofmann, oct–dec 2018.
Calculation algorithms based on demonstrations in the book uFORM iFORM by Ralf Peyn, 2017 and the Laws of Form by George Spencer-Brown, 1969–2015.

Enter forms in parenthesis notation (…) as well as constants (0 unmarked, 1 marked, 2 undetermined, 3 imaginary), variables (single characters: a,b,c,… or labels, using quotes: "Ball", "door is open", "3×2=12", …) and unclear forms (using slashes: /God/, /World Peace/, …). See further examples below.

  • Update: you can now have subscripts in variable names by using the underscore character, e.g. "var_n+1" will display as   varn+1 .

Self-equivalent re-entry forms with nested arguments (as described in the book uFORM iFORM by Ralf Peyn) can also be processed by using curly brackets {…} to mark the re-entry form and separating the nested variables/constants/forms with a comma: {a,b,…}. Please note that the order of the variables is: {deepest nesting → shallowest nesting}.

  • Using pipes before the arguments, you can also specify open forms (without the outer cross) {open|a,…} and either even {2r|a,…} or odd {2r+1|a,…} re-entry numbers as well as combinations {2r|open|a,…}.
  • Update: now you can also switch to the alternative interpretation (as described in the book on p.167) using the keyword "alt", like so: {2r|alt|a,b} (order of keywords is irrelevant).

Click "calculate" to compute your form in a 4-valued calculus based on definitions/demonstrations in uFORM iFORM. When there are only constants in your formula, you'll see one output. With variables, all possible interpretations will be computed.

  • Unclear forms are processed as marked inside uFORMs (according to page 48 of the book) and interpreted with an undetermined value.
  • Click "view JSON" to display a JSON-representation of the form that is internally used for visualization and some of the calculation. In this representation, forms with the property "unmarked": true behave like groups and will not be displayed or computed.

Update: visualize forms in a tree or graph representation (circle packing layout).

  • An ideal graph representation of re-entry forms is still being worked on. For now, re-entry forms are marked red and a little red dot inside them (or their nested forms) indicate the point of re-entry. Note that open re-entry forms get an extra nested space with the dot inside to be consistent with the short notation of such forms in uFORM iFORM. Future releases will hopefully replace this temporary design with the more iconic spirals used in the WELTFORM-notation for undetermined forms (suggestions for flexible spiral formulas are very welcome).
  • Depth trees help to analyze the structure of forms in detail and especially clarify what is going on in spaces of a certain depth if one works with more complex forms. Re-entry forms are indicated with a red circle that has no fill when the form is open. Their nested structure that leads to the (red, small) point of re-entry is indicated by red dashed lines and circles. At the end you can see if the form has an even or uneven re-entry number.
  • In graphs, you can also switch to an alternative representation I call "GestaltFORM" (think of figure-ground-relationship in psychological gestalt theory) that helps to distinguish even from uneven depths. It can also reinforce the recognition of form patterns, such as ((a)(b)) (which can be interpreted as the conjunction of a and b in the context of propositional logic).
  • You can also download rendered trees or graphs in an SVG format (which is essentially what they are already).
  • Technical side note: Both, depth trees and graphs, are powered by D3.js for visualization, using my JSON representation format as data.

Notation examples:

  • form: (), (a), (0), (2)3, (ab), ((a)b), ((("a_1")"a_2")…)"a_n", ((/apple/)/banana/)
  • re-entry form (nested variables): {,}, {a,b}, {(a())"2_b",{"more"}1c,d}, {/deeming/,/telling/,/understanding/}
  • re-entry form with even re-entry number: {2r|a,b,…}
  • re-entry form with odd re-entry number: {2r+1|a,b,…}
  • re-entry form without outer cross: {open|a,b,…}
  • re-entry form with alternative interpretation: {alt|a,b,…}
