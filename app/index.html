Calculate

Click ‚Äúcalculate‚Äù to compute your form in a 4-valued calculus based on definitions/demonstrations in uFORM iFORM by Ralf Peyn. When there are only constants or unclear forms in your formula, the calculator will output one value. With variables, all possible interpretations will be computed.1

  • Unclear forms are processed inside uFORMs (see page 48 of the book) and interpreted with an undetermined value.
  1. Please be aware that with each variable you add to your form, there will be exponentially more values to compute and render inside the table (which will become an enormous list and may take forever to display if you add more than six variables). If you are, however, successful in rendering the table, you may want to use the search function of your browser to look for specific interpretations or values. In the future I may also add filtering functionality, so stay tuned.
Side note

Pressing ‚ÄúformDNA‚Äù to the right of the input field will output a string of numbers that I call (obviously) ‚ÄúformDNA‚Äù. It is a code format that stores all possible interpretation results of a FORM in a single quaternary number. It is essentially a compact representation of the value table that you can see by clicking on ‚Äúcalculate‚Äù. For more information about formDNA and its applications, please read my introductory notebook.

Analyze

I have designed two visualzation types that can be used to analyze value patterns and FORM structure:

vmap

vmaps are variable/value-maps and can be seen as an alternative representation of value tables that is especially useful for pattern recognition. Each variable of a FORM is represented as a diamond shaped group that consists of four smaller groups representing its interpretation states in four directions:

  • ‚Üê : 0 (unmarked)
  • ‚Üí : 1 (marked)
  • ‚Üì : 2 (undetermined)
  • ‚Üë : 3 (imaginary)

Following each direction, this process is repeated recursively down to the smallest diamonds (the ones that are actually drawn), which represent the interpretation results. Each result is color-coded according to the color convention used in uFORM iFORM:

Below the diagram you can see the order in which variables are interpreted in the vmap. A different ordering can be selected using the drop-down menu above the diagram. A vmap of a FORM with more than one variable is called a vmap perspective, since it can only display one unique ordering path. It is often useful to examine multiple different perspectives side by side to spot different sub-patterns or symmetries. By clicking on the ‚Äú+‚Äù next to the diagram, you can render all vmap perspectives at once.

Depth Tree

Depth trees help to analyze the structure of forms in detail and especially clarify what is going on in spaces of a certain depth if one works with more complex forms or for students to help to understand these concepts. Re-entry forms are indicated with a red circle that is not filled when the form is open. Their nested structure, that leads to the (tiny red) point of re-entry, is indicated by red dashed lines and circles.

Visualize

You can visualize your forms using one of two different representation types:

Circle Notation

A complete representation of forms using circles instead of the more common hooks.1 In my opinion, this is a more clear and intuitive representation of forms, although not necessarily the most spatially and notationally efficient. Boundaries are clearly separated (contrary to hook-notation), which makes it easier for learners to grasp the concept of forms and hopefully avoid violating the principle of ‚Äúperfect continence‚Äù, making them unclear and thus inoperative.2 Also, this notation does not suggest any kind of reading order3.

Options
  • Using the buttons below the visualization, you can switch to an alternative representation style I call ‚ÄúGestaltFORM‚Äù. It makes use of the figure-ground-relationship discovered by psychological gestalt theory to help you to visually distinguish between even and uneven depths. It can also reinforce the recognition of form patterns, such as ((a)(b)) (which can be interpreted as the conjunction of a and b in the context of propositional logic).
  1. An ideal circle graph representation of re-entry forms is still being worked on. For now, re-entry forms are marked red and a little red dot inside them (or their nested forms) indicate the point of re-entry. Note that open re-entry forms get an extra nested space with the dot inside to be consistent with the short notation of such forms in uFORM iFORM. Future releases will hopefully replace this temporary design with the more iconic spirals used in the WELTFORM-notation for undetermined forms (suggestions for flexible spiral formulas are very welcome).
  2. Everyone who has read uFORM iFORM will know that this was the main point of critique by Ralf Peyn regarding G. Spencer-Brown's Laws of Form".
  3. Since the relation between forms is commutative, there is no need for any specific order between the elements inside a form, which is why a graph-like 2D-representation seems much more natural to me than arranging forms on a straight line. As a demonstration, see page 44 in uFORM iFORM.
Hook Notation

This is the common representation of forms introduced by George Spencer-Brown in his Laws of Form and also used throughout in uFORM iFORM. You may prefer it over circle notation when working mathematically with forms, since it integrates much better with written text and is more spatially efficient. On the downside, it can be much more difficult to read when working with complex forms (as a demonstration, try ((((((a)b)a)(((a)b)(b)))a)b) and compare both representations).
